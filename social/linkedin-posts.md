## Post 1 — From "The New Currency of Professional Success"
*Source: https://siwan.io/blog/perception-beats-qualification*
*Angle: Career relevance in the AI era — uncomfortable truth, framework, engagement question*

---

Here's something nobody in your industry wants to say out loud:

A 22-year-old who posts consistently about what they're building with AI has more professional leverage right now than a 15-year veteran who keeps their expertise entirely offline.

The veteran might be more skilled. More experienced. Better at the actual work.

It doesn't matter.

Because the new gatekeepers aren't hiring managers reviewing resumes. They're audiences. Communities. People who've been quietly deciding who the experts are based on who shows up, who teaches, and who leads the conversation.

This isn't opinion. It's the mechanism of how influence works now.

AI changed the game in a specific way: it compressed the execution layer. The skills that took years to develop — writing production code, generating polished presentations, analyzing complex datasets — AI can now replicate at 80% quality in 8% of the time.

So the question is no longer *can you do it?*

The question is now *do people believe you understand how to get it done?*

That's a fundamentally different game. And most professionals are still playing the old one.

What actually works now:

**Synthesize, don't just execute.** Anyone can generate output with AI. The people who win are the ones who can explain why one approach is better, what the output means in context, and where the technology falls short.

**Show your thinking publicly.** Share what you're trying, what worked, what failed, what tool saved you three hours. People don't follow polished perfection — they follow people who make them feel like they're learning something real.

**Pick a lane and own it.** Being vaguely knowledgeable about everything signals nothing. AI + your specific industry is a position. That's a reason to be followed.

**Show up before the demand arrives.** The people who will win in the next five years are building their audience now, before they need it.

The AI wave is still early. Most industries are still figuring out what it means. The professionals who define what leadership looks like in AI-augmented fields are being decided right now — not by resumes, not by credentials.

By who shows up.

What's your one intersection? The thing you want to be known for at the AI + [your expertise] intersection?

## Post 2 — From "Your CS Degree Isn't Worthless. Your Plan Is."
*Source: https://siwan.io/blog/cs-degree-in-ai-era*
*Angle: CS grads questioning their degree — reassure, reframe, redirect toward builder mindset*

---

Stanford CS grad. 3.8 GPA. $180k in debt. Applied to 847 jobs since January. Zero offers.

A tweet went viral this week. The hiring manager at a Series C startup told him: "We used to have 8 junior engineers. Now we have 2 seniors with Cursor and they ship faster than the old team of 10."

I understand the frustration. But I think the reaction is pointing at the wrong thing.

**The degree didn't fail him. The plan did.**

A real CS education teaches you to decompose problems. Model systems. Think about tradeoffs under constraints. Reason about things you've never seen before.

That skill doesn't expire. It's not being replaced by Cursor or Claude.

What's being replaced is the *job* that skill used to fund entry into.

The junior engineering role was never really about the code. It was about getting paid to learn in a structured environment, surrounded by seniors who shaped your instincts.

AI compressed that on-ramp. Two senior engineers with good tooling now output what ten used to. Companies didn't get rid of the *thinking* — they got rid of the *execution headcount*.

That's a real problem if your plan was: get degree → get job → write code for salary.

That plan is broken. Not the degree.

Here's what I'd say to every CS grad staring at this situation:

You have four years of problem-solving training and the ability to build software. That's not nothing. That's a starting point most people would kill for.

Stop applying to 847 jobs. Start talking to people about problems they have. Build the smallest useful thing. Ship it publicly before it's ready. Learn to write and talk about what you're building.

The skills that matter now: identifying problems, building solutions, distributing them.

Not the act of writing code.

The corporate job was never the goal. It was a comfortable detour.

Now the detour is closed. That's not a tragedy. It's a forcing function.

Are you still looking for the traditional job, or have you started building something of your own?

## Post 3 — From "You Have About Two Years"
*Source: https://siwan.io/blog/ai-agent-window*
*Angle: Business/career urgency — the 2-year window, practical steps, what this looks like in practice*

---

The window for building career-defining advantages in AI is roughly two years.

Not "AI is important" — you already know that. I mean the specific window where being early still gives you disproportionate leverage.

Here's why it closes:

Every platform shift follows the same pattern. A new capability emerges. A small group of early builders captures outsized value. A skills premium appears because demand far outpaces supply.

Then within 3-4 years, the talent pool catches up. The premium compresses. Being early no longer matters.

Mobile apps: 2008-2012. iOS developers commanded 2-3x salaries. By 2013, every CS grad knew mobile development.

We are in year one of that window for AI agents.

**The numbers:**
- AI agent market: $7.84B today → $52.62B by 2030 (46% CAGR)
- AI skills salary premium: 56% more than those without
- Gartner: 40% of enterprise apps will embed AI agents by end of 2026 (up from <5% in 2025)

**What this means practically:**

You don't have to build the next platform. The most accessible opportunity is being measurably better at your existing work because you use these tools fluently. That alone reprices your labor.

For those building: the infrastructure opportunities are enormous. MCP server development for niche verticals. Agent observability tools. Compliance consulting for regulated industries. There are 33 million small businesses in the US with no AI strategy and no one on payroll who knows where to start.

**The skills that compound fastest:**

Domain expertise + AI fluency. Not AI fluency alone. Knowing healthcare + AI, or legal + AI, commands a premium that generic AI knowledge doesn't. The solopreneurs thriving right now are the ones who combined deep vertical knowledge with AI tools.

By 2028, this will be normal. University curricula will cover agentic AI. Every developer will know these tools the way they know React today.

Right now, the gap between people who understand this and those who don't is wide and real.

What are you doing in the next 90 days to build into it?
